## General Principles

### Evidence Hierarchy
1. **Direct evidence** (writing samples) — highest weight
2. **Inferred evidence** (reading comprehension from conversation context) — medium weight
3. **Self-reported evidence** (speaking, listening) — lowest weight, always labeled

### Scoring Philosophy
- Score what you see, not what you hope or fear
- A single brilliant sentence does not make a C2 writer; look for consistency
- A single error does not invalidate high-level proficiency; look for patterns
- Register range (casual → formal → technical) is itself a proficiency indicator
- The absence of L1 transfer errors is notable and should factor into scoring
- Intentional stylistic choices (e.g., lowercase typing, emoji use) are not errors

### Conservative vs Honest Calibration
- **Honest:** Claude's genuine best estimate of where the user would score
- **Conservative:** The floor — the score Claude is highly confident the user would achieve
  or exceed. Typically 0.5 IELTS bands or one CEFR sub-level lower than the honest estimate.

## CEFR Descriptors {#cefr}

### C2 — Mastery
- Can understand virtually everything heard or read with ease
- Can express themselves spontaneously, very fluently and precisely
- Can differentiate finer shades of meaning in complex situations
- No detectable non-native patterns; or patterns so minor they don't impede at all
- **Writing indicators:** Near-native idiom, natural register shifts, sophisticated
  pragmatics, zero or near-zero L1 transfer, complex grammatical structures used
  accurately and naturally

### C1 — Effective Operational Proficiency
- Can understand a wide range of demanding, longer texts and implicit meaning
- Can express ideas fluently and spontaneously without much obvious searching
- Can use language flexibly and effectively for social, academic, and professional purposes
- Can produce clear, well-structured, detailed text on complex subjects
- **Writing indicators:** Strong vocabulary range, mostly natural phrasing, occasional
  minor non-native patterns that don't impede communication, good register control

### B2 — Vantage
- Can understand the main ideas of complex text on concrete and abstract topics
- Can interact with a degree of fluency that makes regular interaction with natives possible
- Can produce clear, detailed text on a wide range of subjects
- **Writing indicators:** Good grammar with occasional errors in complex structures,
  adequate vocabulary for most topics, some non-native phrasing patterns
- **Distinguishing B2 from C1:** B2 writers can handle familiar topics well but may
  struggle with nuance, hedging, or sophisticated pragmatics. Their writing is correct
  but may feel slightly "textbook" — lacking the natural rhythm of C1+ prose.
  Articles, prepositions, and collocations are the most common weak points.

### B1 — Threshold
- Can understand the main points of clear standard input on familiar matters
- Can deal with most situations likely to arise while traveling
- Can produce simple connected text on familiar topics
- **Writing indicators:** Basic grammar mostly correct, limited vocabulary range,
  noticeable L1 transfer patterns, simple sentence structures predominate
- **Distinguishing B1 from B2:** B1 writers stick to simple structures and familiar
  vocabulary. When they attempt complex sentences, errors increase noticeably.
  They can communicate their meaning but the reader does some interpretive work.
  Code-switching or L1 words may appear when vocabulary fails.

### A2 — Waystage
- Can understand frequently used expressions related to basic personal areas
- Can communicate in simple and routine tasks on familiar topics
- **Writing indicators:** Frequent grammar errors, very limited vocabulary, heavy L1
  transfer, mostly simple sentences

### A1 — Breakthrough
- Can understand and use familiar everyday expressions and very basic phrases
- **Writing indicators:** Very basic vocabulary and structures only

### Intermediate Levels
Use "+" notation for strong performance within a level (e.g., "B2+" or "C1+").
Use "approaching" for users near the boundary (e.g., "C1, approaching C2").

## IELTS Academic Bands {#ielts}

### Band 9.0 — Expert User
- Full command of the language; appropriate, accurate, fluent with complete understanding

### Band 8.0–8.5 — Very Good User
- Fully operational command with occasional unsystematic inaccuracies
- Handles complex detailed argumentation well

### Band 7.0–7.5 — Good User
- Operational command with occasional inaccuracies and misunderstandings
- Generally handles complex language well

### Band 6.0–6.5 — Competent User
- Generally effective command despite some inaccuracies and misunderstandings
- Can use and understand fairly complex language

### Band 5.0–5.5 — Modest User
- Partial command, coping with overall meaning in most situations
- Likely to make many mistakes

### Sub-score Guidance (Writing Only — from text evidence)
- **Task Achievement:** Does the writing address its purpose fully?
- **Coherence & Cohesion:** Logical organization, paragraph structure, linking
- **Lexical Resource:** Vocabulary range, precision, naturalness
- **Grammatical Range & Accuracy:** Variety and correctness of structures

### IELTS Score Mapping from Writing Evidence
| Writing Quality | Estimated Writing Band | Estimated Overall (writing-weighted) |
|---|---|---|
| Near-native, sophisticated pragmatics, zero L1 transfer | 8.0–8.5 | 7.5–8.5 (speaking may limit) |
| Strong, natural, minor non-native traces | 7.0–7.5 | 7.0–8.0 |
| Good, occasional errors in complex structures | 6.0–6.5 | 6.0–7.0 |
| Adequate, noticeable L1 patterns | 5.0–5.5 | 5.0–6.0 |

### IELTS General Training vs Academic

The IELTS General Training test uses the same Speaking and Listening sections as Academic,
but has different Reading and Writing tasks:

- **GT Reading** uses everyday English texts (ads, manuals, notices) rather than academic
  passages. For users with strong practical English from diverse real-world contexts
  (emails, support tickets, product reviews), GT Reading scores may be slightly higher
  than Academic Reading scores. Estimate +0.5 band for users whose evidence is heavily
  practical rather than academic.

- **GT Writing Task 1** is a letter (formal, semi-formal, or informal) rather than a
  graph/chart description. Users who demonstrate strong register control and polite
  correspondence skills in their evidence may score slightly higher on GT Writing.
  The band descriptors and scoring criteria are otherwise identical.

- **Overall band calculation** is the same: average of four components, rounded to
  nearest 0.5.

When including IELTS GT alongside Academic, note the differences in the score card
descriptions rather than duplicating identical information.

## TOEFL iBT Score Ranges {#toefl}

Total: 0–120 (four sections, each 0–30)

### Section Mapping from Writing Evidence
| Section | What to Estimate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Reading (0–30) | Inferred from comprehension in conversations | If user understands complex topics without confusion, estimate high |
| Writing (0–30) | Direct evidence from writing samples | Primary evidence source |
| Listening (0–30) | Self-reported only | Must be labeled |
| Speaking (0–30) | Self-reported only | TOEFL speaking rubric penalizes pronunciation and fluency heavily |

### TOEFL Writing Score Mapping
| Writing Quality | Estimated Writing Score |
|---|---|
| Near-native, complex structures, precise vocabulary | 28–30 |
| Strong, well-organized, minor issues | 24–27 |
| Good, clear ideas, some grammatical/vocab limitations | 18–23 |
| Adequate, limited range, noticeable errors | 12–17 |

### TOEFL Speaking Considerations
The TOEFL speaking section heavily penalizes:
- Pronunciation that requires listener effort
- Hesitations and self-corrections
- Limited fluency/pace
Even users with excellent grammar and vocabulary may score 22–25 if they have a strong
accent and limited oral practice. Note this in the scorecard when relevant.

## Cambridge Exam Equivalents {#cambridge}

| Exam | CEFR Level | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| CPE (C2 Proficiency) | C2 | Mastery-level; tests nuanced understanding and expression |
| CAE (C1 Advanced) | C1 | Grade A certifies C2 in some skills; Grade C is solid C1 |
| FCE (B2 First) | B2 | Grade A approaches C1; Grade C is solid B2 |
| PET (B1 Preliminary) | B1 | Threshold-level certification |
| KET (A2 Key) | A2 | Basic-level certification |

### Cambridge Mapping from Writing Evidence
- C2-level writing → CPE pass likely; CAE Grade A certain
- Strong C1 writing → CAE Grade A/B; CPE possible pass
- Solid C1 writing → CAE Grade B/C
- B2 writing → FCE Grade A/B; CAE unlikely pass
- B1 writing → PET Grade A/B; FCE unlikely pass

### Use of English Considerations
Cambridge exams include a "Use of English" component testing:
- Vocabulary precision, collocations, phrasal verbs
- Grammatical transformations
- Error identification
Meta-linguistic awareness (user discusses language systematically) is a strong
predictor of success on this component.

### Context-Specific Notes
- TOEIC L&R is the most widely used English test in France, Japan, and South Korea
- French "Grandes Écoles" typically require 785+ for diploma validation
- Many multinational companies set 800+ as a hiring threshold
- The test focuses on business/workplace English
- Very high-level users (C1+) may lose points only to concentration, not comprehension

## Mapping Writing Evidence to Speaking/Listening {#mapping-oral}

### What Writing CAN Tell Us About Other Skills
- **Vocabulary range in writing** is a floor for receptive vocabulary (listening/reading)
- **Grammar accuracy in writing** suggests grammar knowledge that supports comprehension
- **Register awareness** suggests social/pragmatic listening comprehension
- **Idiom use** suggests extensive exposure to native speech

### What Writing CANNOT Tell Us
- Pronunciation quality
- Listening speed / processing under time pressure
- Speaking fluency and automaticity
- Real-time comprehension of varied accents

### Reasonable Inferences
- If writing is at C2, reading is very likely C2 (writing is harder than reading)
- If writing is at C2, listening is likely C1–C2 (supported by self-report)
- If writing is at C2, speaking could be anywhere from B2 to C2 depending on practice
- The gap between writing and speaking is often 1–2 CEFR levels for people who learned
  English primarily through reading/media rather than immersion

## Handling Self-Reported Data {#self-reported}

### Labeling Requirements
Any score based primarily on self-reported evidence must include:
- "(self-reported)" in the skill breakdown
- A note in the methodology that speaking/listening scores are estimated from self-report

### Interpreting Self-Reports
Users tend to:
- **Overestimate** speaking ability (they know what they want to say)
- **Accurately estimate** listening ability (they know if they understood or not)
- **Underestimate** or **accurately estimate** writing (they can see their mistakes)

### Corroborating Self-Reports
Look for internal consistency:
- Claims of "full comprehension of native speech" + C2-level idiom use in writing → credible
- Claims of "fluent speaker" + limited vocabulary in writing → may be overestimated
- Claims of heavy media consumption + natural phrasing in writing → consistent

## Handling Mixed-Level Evidence

Sometimes writing quality varies significantly across samples. Before averaging, investigate:

### Common Explanations for Variability
- **Topic familiarity:** Users write better about subjects they know deeply
- **Register mismatch:** Casual chat may look "lower" than formal writing — this is normal
  and actually demonstrates register awareness (a positive signal)
- **Time pressure / context:** Quick replies vs. considered responses
- **Fatigue or distraction:** One bad sample among many good ones is noise, not signal
- **Intentional informality:** Lowercase, abbreviations, emoji in casual contexts are
  stylistic choices, not errors (confirm with the user)

### How to Score
- Weight the **best sustained performance** most heavily — this reflects actual competence
- Note the **range** if it's significant (e.g., "Writing ranges from B2 in unfamiliar
  technical domains to C2 in areas of expertise")
- If variability is extreme (e.g., some writing looks A2 and some looks C1), investigate
  whether third-party text was accidentally included in the corpus
- The callout section is a good place to contextualize variability for the reader

## Common L1 Transfer Patterns by Language

These are not exhaustive, but help Claude know what to look for based on the user's
native language. The **absence** of expected transfer patterns is itself notable evidence.

**Critical framing rule:** A pattern is only a *transfer signal* when it appears
**involuntarily and invariantly** — i.e., the user can't or doesn't shift away from it
even when context calls for a different register. If the user deploys the pattern in
some contexts and not others, it's more likely a **conscious style choice** or
**cultural norm**, which is a sign of pragmatic competence, not L1 interference.

For example: a user who writes formally in professional emails and casually in chat
is demonstrating register control. A user who writes with the same level of formality
in a hotel inquiry and a casual message to a friend may be showing L1 transfer — or
may simply prefer a more formal personal style. British English norms, for instance,
are generally more formal than American English norms, and neither is "more correct."
Claude should not penalize formality unless it is clearly inappropriate for the context
**and** the user shows no evidence of being able to shift down when needed.

### French → English
- Article overuse or misuse ("the life is beautiful")
- Preposition calques ("depend of" instead of "depend on")
- False friends (actuellement ≠ actually, éventuellement ≠ eventually)
- Adjective placement after noun ("a car red")
- Present tense for ongoing states ("I am here since 3 years")
- Overuse of formal discourse markers ("Let us consider", "To conclude") — but ONLY
  flag this as transfer if the user cannot shift to informal register when context demands it.
  French written culture rewards precision and structure; a user who writes precisely by
  choice is not showing interference. Look for evidence of register range before scoring.

### Spanish → English
- Subject pronoun omission
- Adjective placement ("the house white")
- Ser/estar distinction bleeding into English tense choices
- Double negatives
- Preposition misuse ("in the morning" vs "at the morning")

### Mandarin/Cantonese → English
- Article omission (no articles in Chinese)
- Tense marking omission or inconsistency
- Plural marking omission
- Topic-comment structure ("This restaurant, the food is good")
- Verb complement patterns

### Japanese/Korean → English
- SOV word order traces
- Article and plural omission
- Politeness level transfers — but note that high politeness in Japanese/Korean speakers
  may reflect cultural norms that are deliberately maintained, not inability to shift register.
  Same principle as the French formality note: only flag if invariant and contextually inappropriate.
- Relative clause positioning

### German → English
- Verb-second / verb-final traces in complex sentences
- False friends (bekommen ≠ become)
- Compound noun creation patterns
- Capitalization of nouns

### Arabic → English
- Definite article overuse
- Relative clause structure differences
- Coordination preference over subordination
- Right-to-left punctuation habits in digital text

Note: This list covers common patterns only. For other L1s, Claude should research
typical transfer patterns if web search is available, or note that L1-specific analysis
was not performed.
